## Our Legal Duty

We are required by law to protect the privacy of your health information. We call this information "protected health information," or PHI. PHI includes things like your medical records, treatment history, and billing information.

We must:
- Keep your health information private
- Give you this notice about our privacy practices
- Follow the terms described in this notice
- Tell you if your information is ever accessed or shared in a way it should not have been

---

## How We Use and Share Your Health Information

We use and share your health information to provide you with services. The following explains when and why we may do this.

### For Your Treatment

We may share your health information with doctors, nurses, counselors, case managers, and other staff involved in your care. For example, a counselor may share information with a nurse or medical provider to make sure you receive the right services.

### For Payment

If you receive benefits through Medicaid, Medicare, insurance, or another program, we may share your health information to get payment for the services you receive. For example, we may send a claim that includes details about your diagnosis or treatment.

### For Health Care Operations

We may use your health information to run our programs and improve the quality of care we provide. This includes:
- Training staff and volunteers
- Reviewing records to ensure care is meeting standards
- Conducting audits or compliance reviews
- Planning and managing our services

### As Required by Law

We may share your health information when we are required to do so by federal, state, or local law. This includes situations such as:
- Reporting certain communicable diseases to public health authorities
- Complying with a court order or subpoena
- Responding to oversight audits by government agencies

### To Prevent a Serious Threat to Safety

If we believe, in good faith, that sharing your information is necessary to prevent a serious and immediate threat to your health or safety, or the health or safety of another person, we may share your information with appropriate individuals or authorities.

### Other Permitted Uses and Disclosures

In some cases, the law allows or requires us to share your information without your written permission. These situations include:
- Workers' compensation claims
- Public health and safety reporting
- Law enforcement purposes, within legal limits
- Research, under strict conditions to protect your privacy

## Special Protections for Substance Use Disorder Records

**This section applies if you receive or have received substance use disorder (SUD) treatment services from us.**

Records about your participation in a substance use disorder treatment program are protected by a federal law called **42 CFR Part 2**, in addition to HIPAA. This law gives these records stronger protections than other health information.

What this means for you:

- We **cannot share** your SUD treatment records without your **written consent**, except in very limited circumstances
- We cannot tell anyone, including law enforcement, courts, or employers, that you are or were in a SUD program, unless you give us written permission or the law specifically requires it
- Your SUD records **cannot be used to investigate or prosecute you** for a crime without a court order that meets very strict legal requirements

Exceptions where we may share SUD records without your consent include:
- A medical emergency that requires immediate treatment
- Communications among staff within our program who need the information to provide your care
- Audit or oversight of our program by qualified entities, under strict confidentiality rules
- A court order that specifically authorizes disclosure and meets the requirements of 42 CFR Part 2
- Reporting child abuse or neglect where required by state law

If you want us to share your SUD records with anyone outside our program, we will ask you to sign a written consent form that explains exactly what information will be shared, with whom, and for what purpose. You may revoke that consent at any time.

## Your Rights

You have the following rights regarding your health information. To exercise any of these rights, contact our Privacy Officer (see contact information at the end of this notice).

### Right to See and Get a Copy of Your Records

You have the right to look at and get a copy of your health records. You must submit your request in writing. We may charge a reasonable fee for copying. In some limited situations, we may deny your request. If we deny it, we will tell you why and explain your options.

### Right to Ask Us to Correct Your Records

If you believe that information in your records is incorrect or incomplete, you may ask us to change it. You must make this request in writing and explain why you believe the information should be changed. We may deny your request, but we will explain why.

### Right to a List of Disclosures

You may ask for a list of the times we have shared your health information with others outside our organization, and why. This list will not include disclosures for treatment, payment, or health care operations, or disclosures you already authorized in writing. We will provide this list at no charge once per year. The list covers disclosures made in the past six years.

### Right to Request Limits on How We Use Your Information

You may ask us to limit how we use or share your health information. For example, you may ask us not to share certain information with a family member. We are not always required to agree, but we will consider your request carefully and tell you our decision.

### Right to Request Confidential Communications

You may ask us to contact you only in certain ways or at certain locations. For example, you may ask us to call only your cell phone, or to mail information to an address other than your home. We will accommodate reasonable requests.

### Right to a Paper Copy of This Notice

You may ask for a paper copy of this notice at any time, even if you previously agreed to receive it electronically.

### Right to File a Complaint

If you believe your privacy rights have been violated, you have the right to file a complaint. You will not be penalized or retaliated against for filing a complaint.
